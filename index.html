My name is Xilin (/'ʃi-lin/). I am currently a Staff Engineer at Qualcomm Inc. I obtained my Ph.D. degree under the supervision of Prof. Jan Van der Spiegel (IEEE Life Fellow) at the University of Pennsylvania (Penn) in 2017. 

I am passionate about electronics that interface directly with our brains. My research expertise is in developing integrated circuits to restore lost neurological functions due to brain injuries or diseases. My research outcomes to date include 1 book, 12 journal publications (including Nature Electronics, PNAS, IEEE journals, etc.), 12 conference papers, and 4 U.S. patents/invention disclosures. 

I have received academic awards including: the IEEE Solid-State Circuit Society (SSCS) 2015-16 Predoctoral Achievement Award, the Best Student Paper Award (as the first author) of the 2017 International Symposium on Circuits and Systems (ISCAS), the 1st Place Best Paper Award (as the first author) of the 2015 Biomedical Circuits and Systems Conference (BioCAS), and the Best Paper Award (as the first author) of the 2014 ISCAS in the biomedical Track. I was also the recipient of the Student-Research Preview Award of the 2014 IEEE International Solid-State Circuits Conference (ISSCC).

Research Highlights

Overview of my research in sensor-BMI system design. The system is highly configurable with all necessary components for closed-loop neuromodulation experiments. Key blocks include interface and sensing (a-d, m), computation (e-g), learning (h-j) and communication (k-l).


Developed ICs and systems for sensory encoding experiments in freely behaving monkeys. (a) The custom-designed headstage housing the wireless BMI IC and peripheral electronics. (b) Microphotograph of the bidirectional BMI IC with key blocks highlighted. (c) Wireless sensor interface IC for measuring joint-angle. (d) Miniature wireless force sensor designed in standard CMOS with post-fab, to be implanted under fingertips. The system successfully demonstrated continuous restoration of sensation through modulated micro-stimulation.
