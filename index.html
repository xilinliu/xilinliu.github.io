My name is Xilin (/'ʃi-lin/). I am currently a Staff Engineer at Qualcomm Inc. I obtained my Ph.D. degree under the supervision of Prof. Jan Van der Spiegel (IEEE Life Fellow) at the University of Pennsylvania (Penn), USA. 

My research lies at the intersection of integrated circuits (IC) design and biomedical electronics. I am passionate about developing brain-machine interface (BMI) systems for clinical diagnosis and therapies, fundamental neuroscience research, as well as human-machine interaction. My research leverages and advances knowledge in the areas of integrated circuits, system, and data science. I am excited about the potentials of the BMI technologies in advancing our knowledge of brains and enhancing human life quality.  

My research outcomes to date include 1 book, 12 journal publications (including Nature Electronics, PNAS, IEEE journals, etc.), 12 conference papers, and 4 U.S. patents/invention disclosures. My industrial experience includes contributions to a series of top-tier IC products including the world's first commercial 5G chipset. I have received the IEEE Solid-State Circuit Society (SSCS) 2015-16 Predoctoral Achievement Award. My first author papers have received 3 Best Papers Awards on top conferences. I was also the recipient of the Student-Research Preview Award of the 2014 IEEE International Solid-State Circuits Conference (ISSCC).

Research Overview

Communicating with brains directly using Brain-Machine Interfaces (BMIs) is among the most thrilling technological advancements in our era. Recent years, BMI technologies have shown promising applications in neural prosthetics and clinical diagnosis, as well as treatments of medicine-resistant diseases. However, significant obstacles and challenges remain in this area. For example, most studies to date rely upon open-loop (either recording or stimulation) experiments with wired instrumentation, which significantly limits the experimental methods. The key goal of my research is to conquer these limitations by developing closed-loop, bidirectional, wireless BMIs for unrestrained animal experiments. Through close collaboration with neuroscientists, I’ve been among the first to develop wireless sensor-BMI systems for closed-loop sensorimotor neuromodulation. My work, the PennBMBI, has become a popular benchmark in this emerging area. Novel experiments on freely behaving animals (including monkeys and rats) enabled by the PennBMBI system have successfully uncovered neuroscience mechanisms of active sensing strategies, sensation restoration, and memory consolidation. I’ve also developed wireless neuroprosthetics for augmenting perception and restoring sensation through modulated stimulation.

Projects

Project: Closed-loop Sensory Encoding
Having dexterous hands defines human. Unfortunately, stroke, spinal cord injuries, and neurological diseases rob 5.4 million people of this essential human characteristic in the United States. Recent decades have seen significant advancements in prosthetics technologies, including researches in restoring motor functions using BMIs. However, restoring motor functions alone only solves half of the problem for these patients, because motor movements reply on continuous sensory feedback. To fill this gap, my research focuses on restoring lost sensations. To achieve this goal, I have developed novel IC and system for sensory encoding via closed-loop neuromodulation. The developed device and system have been successfully validated in animal models.
